Understanding Hyperpigmentation & Melasma

Through an Ayurvedic Lens: Causes, Early Signs & Holistic Care

By Dr. Simaran Shahmadar, BAMS, MD (Kayachikitsa)  |  In collaboration with Palette Earth

You notice it gradually — a patch near your cheekbone, a shadow above your upper lip, a darkening around your temples. You blame the sun, or stress, or the last pregnancy. But what if these marks are your skin signalling something deeper — about your inner balance and your doshas?

Hyperpigmentation is among the most common and most misunderstood skin concerns today. Modern dermatology explains it at the cellular level; Ayurveda goes further — asking not just what is happening in the skin, but why it is happening in the body.

What is Hyperpigmentation — and What is Melasma?

Hyperpigmentation refers to patches of skin that become darker than the surrounding area — caused when melanocytes (pigment-producing cells) overproduce melanin. It ranges from mild post-acne marks and sun spots to persistent, patterned darkening.

Melasma is a specific, stubborn form — symmetrical brown or greyish-brown patches on the cheeks, forehead, upper lip, and chin. Strongly linked to hormonal shifts (hence common in pregnancy — the 'mask of pregnancy' — and with oral contraceptives), it is significantly worsened by sun exposure.

In Ayurveda, both correlate with Vyanga and Nilika (Charaka and Sushruta Samhitas). Sushruta describes Vyanga as a shyava (bluish-black), flat, painless facial patch from aggravated Vata-Pitta; Charaka links Pitta mixing with Rakta — the blood-heat connection underlying all hyperpigmentation.

The Ayurvedic Understanding: Dosha, Dhatu & Deeper Causes

Ayurveda places hyperpigmentation in the domain of Pitta dosha (heat and metabolism) and Rakta dhatu (blood tissue). When Pitta is aggravated, it disturbs Rakta quality, driving inflammatory changes that surface as discolouration and uneven tone.

Simultaneous Vata imbalance disrupts nutrient delivery to Twak (skin), causing dryness and dullness. Kapha involvement leads to stagnation — explaining why some pigmentation is deep-seated and slow to shift.

Charaka's Ekadeshiya Shotha — a localised Pitta-Rakta inflammation — maps directly to PIH: the dark mark left after a pimple, and why it lingers.

Early Signs People Often Ignore

Pigmentation rarely appears overnight. The skin signals early — long before visible patches form:

  • A dull complexion that doesn't respond to moisturiser
  • Uneven skin tone, especially after sun exposure
  • PIH marks that take unusually long to fade
  • A persistent shadow on the upper lip or under the eyes
  • Skin that feels rough or dehydrated even in humid weather

Ayurvedically, these signal disturbed Bhrajaka Pitta — the sub-dosha governing skin lustre.

✦ From the Clinic

Patients often describe 'sudden' melasma — but on detailed history, the skin has been signalling for months: dullness, slow PIH recovery, a 'shadow' dismissed as tiredness. Caught at that stage, it is far easier to manage. A common misconception is that facial pigmentation needs only a topical fix. In hormonal cases — PCOS, post-pregnancy — that gives inconsistent results. Understanding the root makes the journey clearer.

Root Causes: What Triggers Pigmentation?

Trigger 01
Hormonal Changes

Pregnancy, PCOS, and contraceptives directly stimulate melanocytes. Ayurveda frames this as Artava dushti — a Pitta-Vata imbalance in reproductive physiology.

Trigger 02
Sun Exposure

UV radiation is the single biggest aggravator. Ayurveda lists Atapa sevana (excess sun) as a direct cause of Vyanga — even brief exposure can undo months of care.

Trigger 03
Diet, Lifestyle & Stress

Spicy, fermented foods and alcohol aggravate Pitta; irregular sleep disturbs Rakta. Chronic stress aggravates both Vata and Pitta — elevated cortisol deepens melanocyte activity over time.

Do's & Don'ts for Pigmentation-Prone Skin

Do
✓ Use gentle cleansers — harsh soaps worsen PIH
✓ Use Amba Haldi, Papaya enzymes, Rose extracts
✓ Prioritise 7–8 hours sleep; practice Pranayama daily
✓ Apply broad-spectrum sunscreen every morning, cloudy days included
✓ Reapply every 2–3 hours outdoors; use hats or scarves
Don't
✕ Over-exfoliate or pick blemishes — both worsen pigmentation
✕ Consume alcohol, spicy food, or skip meals — all aggravate Pitta
✕ Rely on sunscreen alone — UV avoidance is equally essential
✕ Skip protection indoors — UVA penetrates glass and clouds equally
✦ From the Clinic

The most common mistake: skipping sunscreen indoors or on cloudy days. UVA — the primary melasma driver — penetrates glass and cloud equally; I've seen well-progressing cases relapse after one unprotected drive. Fermented foods, achaar, and daily chai quietly sustain Pitta aggravation — feeding the fire darkening the skin.

Topical Care vs. Internal Care

Topical care works for
Mild, superficial tanning
Fresh post-acne marks
Early hyperpigmentation in outer layers
Maintaining an already-corrected tone
Internal support for
Deep, hormonal, or recurring pigmentation
Linked to PCOS, thyroid, or chronic stress
Where topicals alone have fallen short
Systemic Pitta-Rakta imbalance

The two complement each other — when topicals fall short, the missing piece is usually internal.

A Supportive Nighttime Ritual for the Skin

Night is when the skin does its deepest repair. Ayurveda's Ratri charya — the nighttime routine — is built on this principle: the evening Vata phase calls for nourishment. The classical emphasis on Snehana (oleation) gives the skin lipid nourishment to restore Twak and support renewal during sleep.

When Hyperpigmentation is Stubborn: Going Deeper

When hyperpigmentation — particularly melasma — doesn't respond to topicals, it isn't a product failure. It's often a sign that the root sits deeper than the skin's surface.

Herbs on the Rakta Prasadana (blood purification) and Pitta Shamana axis — Manjishtha, Sariva, Guduchi — offer meaningful internal support. For hormonal pigmentation linked to PCOS, addressing Artava Dushti alongside topical care gives more lasting results.

✦ From the Clinic

The best results come when patients pair topical care with modest internal support — a Pitta-calming diet, Manjishtha formulations, better sleep. When the internal environment shifts, the skin responds in ways topicals alone rarely achieve. Almost every case of an inconsistent serum traces back to inadequate sun protection or an unaddressed trigger like PCOS or chronic stress — identifying that is where Ayurveda's individualised lens shines.

✦   ✦   ✦
"Hyperpigmentation and melasma are not simply cosmetic concerns — they are signals. Signals of Pitta aggravation, of Rakta asking for a little cooling, of a body and lifestyle that could use some gentler attention."

Ayurveda doesn't promise overnight results — it offers something more enduring: a way of understanding your skin as part of you, not a problem to fix. A good serum, consistent sun protection, and awareness of your internal triggers will take you further than any single product.

Care for your skin the way you would care for anything worth keeping — consistently, gently, and with attention to what lies beneath.
